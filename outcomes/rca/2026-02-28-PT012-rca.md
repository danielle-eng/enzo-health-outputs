## Contributing Factors Analysis

### Patient Factors

1. **Limited Health Literacy and Medication Understanding:** Patient demonstrated poor understanding of diuretic therapy rationale; perceived side effects (frequent urination) as a reason to reduce dosing without consulting clinician. This reflects broader knowledge gap about CHF pathophysiology and medication management.

2. **Social Support Gaps:** Lives alone with only weekly caregiver visits; lacks daily support structure to monitor symptoms, reinforce medication compliance, or assist with meal planning (fluid/sodium restriction).

3. **Medication Adherence Barriers:** Patient reducing Lasix from BID to once daily due to bothersome side effects without clinical authorization; suggests patient prioritizing comfort over disease management.

4. **Cognitive or Depression Indicators:** No assessment documented, but patient behaviors (med non-adherence, dietary non-compliance, minimization of symptoms) suggest possible depression or cognitive decline affecting self-management.

5. **Denial of Disease Severity:** Patient repeatedly minimized weight gains and symptom escalation despite objective evidence of decompensation; "cheating on fluid restriction" suggests patient did not internalize need for adherence.

---

### Clinical Care Factors

1. **Inadequate CHF Monitoring Protocol:** While RN visits were scheduled 2x/week, assessments were not standardized:
   - 01/26 visit: Weight gain of 3 lbs identified but not escalated as critical sign
   - 02/02 visit: Compliance barrier identified ("skipping doses") but documented as educational opportunity only, not escalated
   - 02/09 visit: 7-lb weight gain (+4% body weight in 2 weeks) documented but still not escalated to physician

2. **Failed Escalation at Multiple Checkpoints:**
   - 01/26 (+3 lbs): should trigger physician contact and possible diuretic adjustment
   - 02/02 (admitted non-adherence): should prompt phone assessment and medication reconciliation with cardiologist
   - 02/04 (telephonic SOB report): should trigger same-day RN visit, not just phone advice
   - 02/09 (+7 lbs, dyspnea increased, orthopnea present): clear signs of decompensation; should have triggered urgent physician notification and consideration of same-day visit or ED referral

3. **Lack of Care Coordination with Primary Cardiology:** No evidence of communication with cardiologist managing CHF between admission (01/08) and hospitalization (02/11). Diuretic adjustments should have involved cardiology, not just nursing judgment.

4. **Reactive vs. Proactive Care Model:** RN approach was reactive (respond to patient-reported symptoms) rather than proactive (weight monitoring, trend analysis, preventive escalation). Only 4 RN visits in 34 days (1 visit per 8.5 days) may be insufficient for newly-admitted CHF patient.

5. **Incomplete Medication Reconciliation:** SOC care plan lists medications but no documented verification that patient actually possesses medications, understands dosing, or has access/financial means to fill prescriptions. Post-discharge medication changes from acute hospital stay may not have been updated in HH record.

6. **Absence of Remote Monitoring:** No structured daily weight monitoring via phone/app; patient self-reporting weight changes only at RN visits (1-2 week intervals). Daily weights via simple scale + phone check-in could have detected 3-7 lb weight gains within 24-48 hours.

### Communication Factors

1. **Provider-Patient Communication Failures:**
   - Patient stated reason for reducing Lasix ("bathroom trips") was a known side effect; no prior education documented on managing side effect vs. reducing medication
   - Patient admitted to "cheating" on diet but no motivational assessment or barriers discussion documented
   - No exploration of why patient was non-compliant (side effects, cost, confusion, depression)

2. **RN-Physician Communication Failure:**
   - RN documented compliance barrier (02/02) but did not contact physician for intervention
   - RN documented severe weight gain (02/09) but documented only "patient education reinforced" without escalation
   - RN received telephonic report of severe dyspnea (02/10) but did not contact on-call physician for urgent assessment order

3. **Caregiver Communication Gaps:**
   - Weekly caregiver visits could have caught medication non-adherence and weight gain; no evidence of coordinated communication between RN and caregiver
   - Caregiver not trained on red flags (weight gain >2-3 lbs, new dyspnea, orthopnea) that trigger emergency contact

4. **Discharge Communication from Acute Hospital:**
   - RN SOC assessment (01/12) does not reference discharge summary or medication changes from recent hospitalization
   - Unclear whether RN reviewed post-discharge medication list or verified patient understanding

### System Factors

1. **Absence of CHF-Specific Monitoring Protocol:** Home health does not have standardized CHF patient monitoring guidelines (daily weights, weekly trend analysis, escalation criteria for weight gain or symptom changes).

2. **Weight Monitoring System Gaps:**
   - No structured daily or twice-weekly weight monitoring via phone/app
   - No automated alerts for weight gains ≥3 lbs in 3 days or ≥5 lbs in 1 week
   - RN visits separated by 1-2 weeks, missing critical window for early intervention

3. **Insufficient Visit Frequency for High-Risk Patient:** 2x/week RN visits may be appropriate for stable CHF but inadequate for newly-admitted patient with recent hospitalization and demonstrated non-adherence risk. Standard of care for post-hospitalization CHF is often 2-3x/week for first 4 weeks.

4. **Lack of Physician Orders for Escalation:** Care plan does not include physician-authorized escalation criteria or standing orders for diuretic adjustment based on weight gain. RN is expected to call physician (reactive) rather than follow protocol (proactive).

5. **No Telehealth/Remote Monitoring Capability:** Home health does not have structured telephonic assessment or telehealth tool for urgent symptom evaluation; reliant on in-person visits.

6. **Caregiver Notification System Absent:** No formal protocol for RN to communicate directly with weekly caregiver about patient status, barriers, or red flags; caregiver may not be aware of medication non-adherence or weight changes.

7. **Documentation Standards Gap:** OASIS and care plan do not include explicit CHF monitoring parameters or escalation thresholds; RNs use clinical judgment without standardized protocol, leading to inconsistent escalation decisions.

## Primary Preventable Factors

**Classification: LIKELY PREVENTABLE**

This hospitalization for acute CHF decompensation was likely preventable. The chain of events shows clear opportunities for intervention:

1. **01/26 Visit (Weight +3 lbs):** Should have triggered same-day or next-day physician contact and possible diuretic adjustment (Preventable via escalation protocol).

2. **02/02 Visit (Admitted non-adherence):** Patient-identified barrier (frequent urination) should have prompted:
   - Physician call to discuss diuretic timing (earlier in day), or dosing adjustment
   - Medication education on rationale and side effect management
   - Increased visit frequency (shift to 3x/week)
   (Preventable via care plan modification and physician engagement)

3. **02/04 Telephonic Call (SOB report):** Should have triggered same-day RN reassessment and vital sign assessment, not just phone advice (Preventable via escalation protocol).

4. **02/09 Visit (Weight +7 lbs, dyspnea +2 points, orthopnea new):** Objective signs of acute decompensation; should have prompted:
   - Immediate physician notification
   - Consideration of urgent diuretic adjustment or ED referral
   - Same-day visit rather than waiting for next scheduled appointment
   (Preventable via escalation protocol and care plan escalation criteria)

5. **02/10 Evening Call (Severe dyspnea, orthopnea):** RN advised patient "call if worsens" but did not arrange urgent assessment or physician contact; early morning ED admission suggests patient decompensated overnight, likely preventable with urgent RN visit 02/10 evening (Preventable via 24/7 triage protocol).

**Not Preventable Factors:**
- CHF is a chronic progressive disease; exacerbations are inherent to condition
- Some patients have biological triggers (dietary indiscretion, infection, arrhythmia) not entirely preventable
- Patient's underlying ejection fraction (35-40%) indicates advanced disease with higher exacerbation risk

**However:** With standardized monitoring, escalation protocols, and physician engagement, this specific decompensation could have been managed outpatient with adjusted diuretics rather than requiring hospitalization. Early detection (01/26 or 02/02) combined with prompt physician intervention likely prevents 02/11 admission.

## Recommendations

### 1. Implement Structured CHF Monitoring Protocol (Immediate)

**Action:** Develop and deploy standardized CHF patient monitoring protocol for all heart failure patients within 2 weeks.

**Protocol Elements:**
- **Daily Weights:** Patient instructed to weigh daily at same time; report weights to RN via phone/app/text
- **Escalation Thresholds:**
  - Weight gain ≥2-3 lbs in 1 day = call RN immediately
  - Weight gain ≥5 lbs in 3 days = same-day or next-day RN visit + physician notification
  - New or worsening dyspnea, orthopnea, or ankle edema = same-day RN visit
- **Weekly RN Assessment:** Review weight trend, vital signs, orthopnea status, compliance, dietary adherence
- **Physician Escalation:** RN notifies cardiologist/PCP within 4 hours of identifying weight gain or symptom escalation; standing orders for diuretic adjustment may be pre-authorized

**Expected Outcome:** Early identification of decompensation signs within 24-48 hours vs. 5-7 day delay; reduction in CHF hospitalizations estimated 30-40%.

---

### 2. Increase Visit Frequency for Post-Hospitalization CHF Patients (30 days)

**Action:** Establish policy requiring 3x/week RN visits for first 4 weeks post-hospital discharge for any CHF patient; then reassess to 2x/week if stable.

**Rationale:**
- Patient PT0012 was admitted 01/08 (day 1 post-discharge from acute hospital)
- First sign of decompensation appeared 01/26 (day 18)
- With 3x/week visits, RN would have seen patient on ~01/23 or 01/25, catching weight gain same week vs. week 3

**Expected Outcome:** Early intervention window increases from 1-2 weeks to 3-4 days; allows rapid adjustment of diuretics before acute decompensation.

---

### 3. Establish Physician-RN Escalation Protocol for CHF (High Priority — 30 days)

**Action:** Create standing orders and communication protocol enabling RN to escalate CHF concerns to cardiologist/PCP without delay.

**Protocol Elements:**
- RN documents finding (weight gain, dyspnea escalation, orthopnea, edema change) in EHR
- RN calls cardiologist/PCP office within 4 hours with brief clinical summary
- Physician may authorize standing order for diuretic increase (e.g., "if weight gain ≥5 lbs, increase Lasix dose by 20mg daily for 3 days, then reassess")
- RN scheduled for follow-up visit within 1-2 days to assess response
- If no improvement or worsening, urgent ED referral discussed

**Expected Outcome:** Patient PT0012 would have had physician contact by 01/26 (day 1 of weight gain); diuretic adjustment by 01/27; reassessment by 01/30; likely prevention of 02/11 hospitalization.

---

### 4. Implement Medication Adherence Assessment and Barrier Mitigation (30 days)

**Action:** Add structured medication adherence assessment to CHF care plan; identify and address barriers (side effects, cost, confusion, depression).

**Assessment Elements:**
- SOC visit: Assess patient understanding of each medication (name, dose, frequency, reason)
- Follow-up visits: Ask open-ended question about medication-taking ("Tell me how you've been taking your water pills") vs. closed question ("Are you taking your Lasix?")
- Identify and document barriers: Side effects, cost, complicated regimen, depression, cognitive decline
- Develop targeted interventions:
  - Side effect: Adjust timing (Lasix earlier in day to minimize night urination), or discuss with physician alternative agents
  - Cost: Assess insurance coverage, recommend generic alternatives, refer to patient assistance programs
  - Complexity: Simplify regimen if possible (e.g., extended-release Lasix once daily instead of BID)
  - Depression: Screen for depression; refer to behavioral health

**Expected Outcome:** Patient PT0012 identified on 02/02 as non-adherent due to side effect; intervention could have included dose timing adjustment (Lasix 8 AM instead of BID) or discussion with cardiologist on alternative diuretics, preventing decompensation.

---

### 5. Establish Caregiver Communication Protocol (30 days)

**Action:** Develop structured communication between RN and weekly caregiver to ensure alignment on monitoring and escalation.

**Protocol Elements:**
- RN calls caregiver weekly to review: Weight trend, medication adherence, symptoms, dietary adherence
- Caregiver trained on red flags: Weight gain >2-3 lbs, new dyspnea, orthopnea, ankle swelling
- Caregiver instructed to call RN immediately if red flags identified; provide RN direct phone number
- Monthly care plan review with patient, RN, and caregiver to ensure shared understanding of goals

**Expected Outcome:** Caregiver can catch signs of decompensation (weight, diet non-compliance) between RN visits; serves as extension of RN monitoring.
