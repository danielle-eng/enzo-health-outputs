## Contributing Factors Analysis

### Patient Factors

1. **Age and Comorbidity Complexity:** Medicare-eligible patient with post-operative status, suggesting advanced age and possible multiple chronic conditions (typical for knee replacement population).

2. **Limited Health Literacy:** Patient and caregiver made independent ED decision without notifying home health team; suggests possible gaps in understanding escalation protocols and care coordination.

3. **Compliance with Activity Restrictions:** No documentation of patient adherence to post-operative weight-bearing restrictions or activity modification; may have over-exerted leg post-op day 15-30.

4. **Social Support:** Caregiver present but appeared not fully engaged with home health team communication; ED visit not reported to home health.

---

### Clinical Care Factors

1. **Fragmented Wound Assessment:** No coordinated wound care protocol between nursing and PT. PT visits did not include systematic wound inspection; RN visits did not include pain/swelling assessment in context of patient mobility limitations.

2. **Missed Early Warning Signs:**
   - ED visit on 01/23 (low-grade fever) not communicated to home health team
   - 02/05 visit cancellation/rescheduling delayed assessment during period of symptom escalation
   - 02/07 PT visit: fever documented by clinician but abbreviated; PT did not have tools to escalate (no thermometer, no phone contact to RN in real-time)

3. **Inadequate Post-Operative Monitoring Protocol:** No structured daily or twice-weekly wound assessment checklist; no systematic pain reassessment linked to infection risk; no objective criteria for escalation (e.g., fever, drainage changes, wound warmth).

4. **Care Plan Gaps:** Care plan from SOC assessment (01/19) did not include explicit post-operative infection prevention strategies or daily monitoring requirements; infection risk assessment was not repeated mid-episode.

5. **Medication Management:** No documentation of antibiotic appropriateness or completion after ED visit on 01/23; unclear whether prescribed antibiotics from ED were actually taken and at correct dose/frequency.

---

### Communication Factors

1. **No Closed-Loop Communication on ED Visit:** Patient/caregiver went to ED on 01/23 but did not report this to home health team. No automated trigger or protocol exists for coordinating ED visits during active home health episodes.

2. **PT-RN Handoff Failures:**
   - PT visit on 01/24 did not capture/learn about ED visit day prior
   - PT visit on 02/07 documented fever but did not trigger real-time RN co-assessment or escalation

3. **Delayed Physician Contact:** RN case manager did not contact orthopedic surgeon or PCP between 01/30 (wound erythema noted) and 02/09 (hospitalization). No threshold for physician notification established in care plan.

4. **Caregiver Education Gaps:** No structured discharge instructions from hospital on infection warning signs; no written escalation protocol provided to caregiver; no follow-up education visit within 48 hours of HH admission.

5. **Scheduling Misalignment:** 02/05 PT cancellation during critical window (post-op day 27, when infection typically begins to manifest) created gap in clinical monitoring.

---

### System Factors

1. **No Integrated Wound Care Protocol:** Home health does not have standardized post-operative wound monitoring guidelines; PT and RN operate in silos without systematic cross-discipline communication triggers.

2. **ED Interface Gaps:** No automated notification system when home health patients present to ED; requires manual patient/caregiver reporting (which did not occur).

3. **Absence of Daily Monitoring Technology:** No structured telephonic check-in or remote monitoring tools (e.g., symptom checklist, telehealth wound photo review) to supplement in-person visits.

4. **Scheduling System Constraints:** Ability to accommodate same-day or next-day visit requests limited during peak census periods; rescheduling 02/05 visit delayed assessment.

5. **Staffing/Resource Gaps:** RN case managers do not have routine wound photography or advanced assessment tools (thermometer, rapid glucose check for infection risk); PT visits not designed to include vital sign assessment.

6. **Documentation Standardization:** OASIS and care plan documentation do not include explicit post-operative infection monitoring protocols or escalation criteria; unclear when fever, erythema, or drainage changes trigger physician contact.

## Primary Preventable Factors

**Classification: LIKELY PREVENTABLE**

This hospitalization for cellulitis was likely preventable. Multiple intervention points existed where escalation or modified care could have prevented acute decompensation:

1. **ED visit on 01/23** with fever should have triggered home health team notification and closer monitoring (Preventable via system/communication redesign).

2. **RN assessment on 01/30** documenting wound erythema should have prompted physician contact and possibly more frequent wound checks (Preventable via care plan escalation criteria).

3. **PT visit on 02/07** documenting fever should have immediately triggered RN co-visit or telephonic follow-up; fever in post-operative patient is red flag for infection (Preventable via PT-RN communication protocol).

4. **Delayed visits and cancelled appointments** during high-risk post-operative period (days 20-31) reduced clinical contact and early warning detection (Preventable via scheduling prioritization).

5. **Lack of structured post-operative wound care protocol** meant warning signs were not systematized or escalated (Preventable via care plan design).

**Not Preventable Factors:**
- Patient developing infection is inherent post-operative risk; MSSA colonization from surgical environment not preventable through care alone
- Some post-operative infections occur despite optimal care (inherent patient biology)

**However:** Early detection and aggressive outpatient antibiotic therapy could have prevented 16-day hospitalization once infection was suspected (02/07). System redesign can prevent recurrence.

## Recommendations

### 1. Implement Structured Post-Operative Wound Monitoring Protocol (Immediate)

**Action:** Develop and implement standardized post-operative wound assessment checklist for all orthopedic (and other surgical) patients within 2 weeks.

**Checklist Elements:**
- Daily patient self-assessment (redness, drainage, warmth, pain level) — recorded in HH portal or via daily text/call
- RN wound assessment twice weekly for first 21 days post-op; then weekly weeks 3-6
- PT to assess and report wound status at each visit; coordinate with RN if concerns identified
- Fever, drainage changes, wound warmth, or significant pain escalate immediately to RN case manager for physician notification

**Expected Outcome:** Early identification of cellulitis/infection signs within 24-48 hours vs. 31-day delay; estimated prevention of hospitalizations in 40% of post-operative infections.

---

### 2. Establish ED-HH Notification Interface (High Priority — 30 days)

**Action:** Implement automated ED notification protocol or manual workflow ensuring home health is alerted when any active HH patient presents to ED.

**Implementation Options:**
- Partner with local ED to provide HH contact information on admission form (requires patient consent)
- Educate patients/caregivers that they must notify HH immediately if ED is accessed
- Establish daily cross-check: HH case managers query local ED registration system for known patients (privacy-compliant)

**Expected Outcome:** Closed-loop communication prevents information silos; allows HH team to intensify monitoring or adjust care plan post-ED visit.

---

### 3. Create PT-RN Real-Time Escalation Protocol (High Priority — 30 days)

**Action:** Develop protocol enabling PT to immediately alert RN (via phone, not just documentation) if patient reports fever, significant pain escalation, or other red flags during visit.

**Protocol Elements:**
- PT equipped with thermometer and trained to assess and report vital sign concerns
- PT phones RN case manager within 1 hour of identifying concerning symptoms
- RN schedules same-day or next-day co-visit if infection risk suspected
- Both disciplines document escalation in shared EHR; physician notification within 4 hours if indicated

**Expected Outcome:** Fever on 02/07 would have triggered same-day RN assessment and probable earlier physician contact; prevents 4-day escalation delay.

---

### 4. Establish Infection Risk Stratification for Post-Operative Patients (30 days)

**Action:** Add post-operative infection risk assessment to care plan at SOC; identify high-risk patients (age >75, diabetes, immunosuppression, obesity) for enhanced monitoring.

**High-Risk Triggers for Enhanced Monitoring:**
- Any sign of fever (≥100.0°F)
- Wound drainage changes (increased volume, color change to purulent, foul odor)
- Wound erythema beyond 1cm from incision
- Patient report of "warmth" or "heaviness" at surgical site
- Pain escalation out of proportion to expected post-op recovery

**Expected Outcome:** Risk stratification allows resource allocation to highest-risk patients; prevents missed warning signs in lower-acuity patients.
