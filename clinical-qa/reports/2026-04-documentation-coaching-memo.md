## Overview

A recent QA review of sample visit notes identified three critical documentation gaps that create significant audit and compliance risk. This memo addresses the most common errors and provides specific before/after examples to improve documentation quality and ADR readiness.

---

## ERROR 1: Missing Explicit Skilled Care Justification

### The Problem
Many clinicians document what they did ("wound care performed," "exercises done") but fail to explicitly state **why skilled services are medically necessary**. Payers and auditors require a clear statement that justifies why a licensed professional (nurse, PT, etc.) must provide this care—not an aide or caregiver.

### Example of POOR Documentation
*"Patient had wound dressing changed today. Wound measuring 2 cm x 2.5 cm. Patient tolerated well. Continue current plan."*

**Why This Fails:**
- No explanation of why a skilled nurse must change this dressing
- Implies this could be non-skilled aide care
- Payer will deny as custodial wound care
- Audit finding: "Medical necessity not established"

### Example of GOOD Documentation
*"Skilled nursing is required for sterile assessment of wound healing, detection of infection signs (erythema, drainage changes, odor, warmth), and sterile dressing changes per protocol. This post-ulcer wound (previously 3+ cm depth) requires skilled clinical judgment to evaluate healing trajectory and prevent complications. Dressing change performed with sterile technique. Wound measured 2 cm x 2.5 cm (improved from 3 cm baseline). Minimal serous drainage, slight erythema consistent with normal inflammatory healing phase. Wife instructed on sterile dressing change technique and when to contact RN if drainage increases or odor develops."*

**Why This Works:**
- Opens with clear skilled justification (assessment, detection, sterile technique)
- Explains what a nurse uniquely does that aide cannot
- Provides clinical context (post-ulcer, tracking improvement)
- Demonstrates skilled assessment (interpreting erythema as normal vs. infection)
- Establishes patient safety need

### How to Fix
At the start of your clinical note, add a "Skilled Care Justification" statement that explicitly explains:
1. What skilled problem you're addressing (medication management, wound infection risk, fall risk, respiratory compromise, etc.)
2. What skilled assessment or judgment is required
3. What could go wrong if not monitored by licensed professional

## ERROR 2: No Documentation of Patient Understanding / Verbalization

### The Problem
Clinicians document education provided but fail to document whether the patient **actually understood** what was taught. Statements like "Patient instructed on..." or "Patient appeared to understand" are insufficient. Payers want evidence that patient can demonstrate understanding.

### Example of POOR Documentation
*"Educated patient on daily weights. Explained importance of monitoring for fluid retention. Patient seemed to understand. Continue monitoring."*

**Why This Fails:**
- "Seemed to understand" is vague and not measurable
- No evidence patient actually comprehended the education
- Raises audit question: "How do you know patient understands?"
- If claim denied, you cannot defend the need for skilled education

### Example of GOOD Documentation
*"Patient education provided on daily weight monitoring as indicator of fluid retention. Reviewed proper technique: same scale, same time daily (morning after void, before breakfast), light clothing only. Explained that 3+ lbs gain in 1 day or 5+ lbs in 1 week requires immediate provider contact. Patient performed demonstration: stepped on scale correctly, noted weight, and stated 'I check myself every morning at 7 AM before breakfast and write it down. If I gain 5 pounds in a week, I call the doctor right away.' Wife also present and confirmed understanding, verbalizing that she will monitor patient's weight daily and alert patient if trending upward."*

**Why This Works:**
- Patient demonstrates understanding through direct action (performs scale use correctly)
- Patient verbalizes specific understanding (quotes what patient said)
- Wife engagement documented (caregiver oversight confirmed)
- Specific details show education was thorough, not generic
- Auditor can see patient is engaged and will comply

### How to Fix
For every education topic, add:
1. **What you said:** Specific content taught (not just "patient educated on...")
2. **How patient/caregiver showed they understood:** Did they say something? Demonstrate? Answer questions correctly?
3. **Direct quote or specific statement:** "Patient stated..." or "Wife demonstrated understanding by..."

**Formula:** "Educated patient on [TOPIC]. Covered [SPECIFIC POINTS]. Patient demonstrated understanding by [ACTION/QUOTE]."

## ERROR 3: No Plan of Care Goal Reference or Progress Documentation

### The Problem
Visit notes describe what clinician did but don't link it to the patient's **specific functional goals** from the Plan of Care. Payers and auditors ask: "What is the patient trying to achieve? Is this visit moving them toward that goal?"

Without goal documentation, the visit looks aimless and non-skilled.

### Example of POOR Documentation
*"Patient performed therapeutic exercises. Mobility improved. Pain better today. Continue PT as ordered."*

**Why This Fails:**
- No reference to any POC goals (what is the patient supposed to accomplish?)
- "Improved" and "better" are vague (improved compared to what?)
- No objective evidence of progress (ROM? Distance? Strength?)
- No timeline or end-goal stated
- Auditor cannot determine if continued PT is justified

### Example of GOOD Documentation
*"POC Goal 1: Patient will achieve hip flexion 0-110 degrees within 2 weeks of start of care to restore ability to climb stairs independently. Progress: Today's ROM: Hip flexion 0-100 degrees (baseline at SOC was 0-60 degrees). Patient has improved 40 degrees over 5 visits. On track to achieve goal within target timeframe. Continue therapeutic exercises 3x/week."*

*"POC Goal 2: Patient will ambulate 100 feet with walker and supervision without dyspnea or increased pain by 3-week mark. Progress: Today patient ambulated 80 feet with walker and contact guard; pain remained 3/10 (baseline 7/10). Ambulation distance increased 45 feet from SOC baseline. Gait mechanics improving; patient decreasing reliance on walker for balance. Goal achievable within timeline. Continue current plan."*

**Why This Works:**
- Goals are specific and measurable (ROM target in degrees, distance in feet, timeline stated)
- Baseline documented at SOC; progress compared to baseline with numbers
- Objective measures (ROM, distance, pain scale) show improvement
- Clinician interprets what improvement means ("on track to achieve goal")
- Auditor understands why PT continues: patient is progressing toward functional goals

### How to Fix
For each relevant POC goal:
1. **State the goal explicitly:** Include what patient is trying to achieve and by when
2. **Document baseline:** What was the starting point? (from SOC or prior visit)
3. **Measure today's status:** Use objective measures (ROM degrees, feet of ambulation, strength grades, pain 0-10 scale)
4. **Compare to baseline:** "Baseline X... Today's status Y... Progress = improvement of Z"
5. **Interpret the trend:** "On track / ahead / behind schedule to meet goal"

**Formula:** "POC Goal: [GOAL with timeline]. Progress: Baseline [MEASURE]. Today [MEASURE]. Improvement [QUANTIFIED]. Status: [On track / needs adjustment]."

## Implementation Checklist

Before submitting any visit note, verify you have included:

- [ ] Explicit skilled care justification statement (why skilled professional is needed)
- [ ] Patient/caregiver verbalization of understanding for ALL education topics (direct quotes)
- [ ] Reference to AT LEAST ONE POC goal
- [ ] Objective progress measure compared to baseline (ROM, distance, weight, edema, etc.)
- [ ] Clinical interpretation of findings (not just numbers; what do they mean?)
- [ ] Homebound status documented with functional rationale
- [ ] OASIS-consistent language (dyspnea severity, edema status, functional capacity)
- [ ] Signature, credentials, date, and time
