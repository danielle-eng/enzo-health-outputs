### ⚠️ Deficiencies Found

**Deficiency 1 — Vague Skilled Care Justification**
*Finding:* Note states "Skilled PT required for deconditioning management and functional mobility training in patient with chronic COPD and recent acute exacerbation," but provides no specific clinical justification for why skilled PT is medically necessary at 8 weeks into episode of care. No explanation of why patient cannot be discharged or managed with home exercise program alone. No baseline assessment findings to justify continued skilled intervention.
*Standard:* CMS CoP 484.60 requires documentation of specific skilled services and medical necessity. For PT in COPD patient at 8 weeks, this requires: explanation of why continued skilled PT is needed (not transitioning to self-directed HEP?), functional baseline from SOC to compare to current status, specific mobility/strength goals driving therapy, clinical assessment findings supporting continued need.
*Risk:* AUDIT RISK - HIGH. At 8 weeks into an episode, payers scrutinize justification for continued skilled therapy. Without clear explanation of why patient still requires PT vs. discharge to HEP, denial is likely.
*Required Correction:* Add detailed justification: "Skilled physical therapy is required for continued functional mobility and activity tolerance improvement in 77-year-old patient with chronic COPD complicated by recent acute exacerbation. Patient remains significantly deconditioned at 8 weeks post-admission; baseline ambulation at SOC was [X] feet; current ambulation is 60 feet. Patient requires skilled assessment of exertional dyspnea, breathing mechanics, activity pacing strategies, and progression of home exercise program. Continued supervision needed to ensure patient does not exceed safe exertional limits and to monitor for signs of acute decompensation (dyspnea increase, decreased SpO2, chest pain). Patient not yet ready for discharge as functional ambulation distance remains below community-level function."

**Deficiency 2 — No Baseline Assessment or Functional Comparison**
*Finding:* Note states "Patient able to ambulate 60 feet today" but provides no comparison to:
  - Baseline ambulation distance at SOC (8 weeks ago)
  - Prior visit ambulation distance (to show progress or decline)
  - Expected progression for COPD patient at 8 weeks
  No ROM assessment documented. No strength assessment documented. No exertional dyspnea scale documented (6-minute walk test, Borg scale, or simple dyspnea rating).

*Standard:* Skilled PT documentation requires baseline and comparison data showing functional progression or identifying barriers to progress. For COPD deconditioning, this includes:
  - Ambulation distance with specific assistance level and dyspnea severity
  - Stair negotiation ability (steps completed, assistance needed)
  - Breathing mechanics assessment (pursed-lip breathing, diaphragmatic breathing effectiveness)
  - Exertional dyspnea severity (numeric scale or Borg Rating of Perceived Exertion)
  - Exercise capacity (how much activity before patient requires significant rest)

*Risk:* AUDIT RISK - CRITICAL. Without baseline or comparison data, auditor cannot determine if PT is providing skilled progression toward functional goals or if patient has plateaued and should be discharged. Claim likely to be denied.
*Required Correction:* MANDATORY. Document: "Functional Status Assessment: Ambulation: Patient currently ambulates 60 feet with minimal rest breaks required (baseline at SOC 8 weeks ago: [X] feet). Improvement of [X] feet demonstrates progress toward goal of community ambulation (150+ feet needed for functional community access). Stair negotiation: Patient practiced stair climbing with supervision; able to negotiate [X] stairs with rail and contact guard. Prior visit: [X] stairs. Exertional dyspnea: Patient reports dyspnea severity [numeric rating] during 60-foot ambulation; baseline at SOC was [rating]. Improvement in dyspnea severity with same distance indicates improved conditioning. Breathing mechanics: Patient demonstrates use of pursed-lip breathing at rest and with activity (appropriate energy conservation strategy). Coordination of breathing with activity improving; previously dyspneic with stairs, now breathing coordination improving during stair training."

**Deficiency 3 — Insufficient Exercise Documentation and Home Program Detail**
*Finding:* Note lists exercises (walking, stairs, breathing techniques, activity pacing) but provides no detail on:

  - Number of stairs negotiated or target
  - Breathing technique specifics (pursed-lip vs. diaphragmatic)
  - Patient tolerance to each exercise
  - Modifications made during visit

*Standard:* Skilled PT requires documentation of specific exercises performed with detail on patient response:
  - "Patient ambulated [X] feet today with supervision, demonstrating improved breathing coordination compared to prior visit. Patient now able to ambulate distance without pausing mid-distance (prior required 2 rest breaks for same distance). No increase in dyspnea beyond baseline."
  - "Stair training: Patient practiced ascending [X] flights using rail and stepping pattern; rate appropriate; no excessive dyspnea. Wife present and educated on supervision technique and safety precautions. Wife verbalized understanding of need to allow patient adequate time between steps."

*Risk:* DOCUMENTATION QUALITY - HIGH. Medical record does not reflect what PT specifically did; non-reproducible treatment makes outcome assessment impossible.
*Required Correction:* Document each exercise session with specifics on distance/duration, breathing pattern used, patient tolerance, modifications, and wife's involvement.

**Deficiency 5 — Minimal Vital Signs Interpretation; No Post-Exercise Monitoring**
*Finding:* Vital signs listed at beginning of visit (pre-exercise), but no post-exercise vital signs documented. No interpretation of pre-exercise vitals relative to baseline or exertional capacity. No monitoring of cardiovascular response to therapy.
*Standard:* PT notes for COPD patients should include: pre-exercise vital signs, post-exercise vital signs (to assess cardiopulmonary response to activity), exertional dyspnea severity, and clinical judgment about exercise safety.
*Risk:* SAFETY/DOCUMENTATION RISK - MODERATE. Without post-exercise monitoring, cardiac complications during therapy could be missed. Documentation does not demonstrate appropriate safety monitoring of therapy.
*Required Correction:* Document: "Vital Signs: Pre-exercise: BP 136/80, HR 74, RR 18 at rest, SpO2 94% on room air. Post-exercise (after 60-foot ambulation): BP 140/84, HR 80, RR 20 (appropriate response to exertion), SpO2 93% on room air (slight drop expected with exertion; returned to 94% within 2 minutes of rest). Exertional dyspnea: Patient reports dyspnea severity [rating] during activity, which is below safety threshold. No chest discomfort, no dizziness. Appropriate response to therapy and safe for continued progression."

*Risk:* AUDIT RISK - HIGH. Without measurable goals, auditor cannot determine when patient should be discharged and questions whether visits are medically necessary or habitual.
*Required Correction:* MANDATORY. Add specific goals: "POC Goal 1: Patient will demonstrate improved ambulation capacity from current baseline of 60 feet to 150+ feet with minimal assistance and dyspnea ≤2/10 within 3 weeks, enabling safe community ambulation. Progress to date: Currently at 60 feet; on track for goal if [specific improvement rate maintained]. POC Goal 2: Patient will negotiate one flight of stairs independently with rail within 2 weeks. Progress to date: Currently negotiating stairs with supervision; demonstrating appropriate progression. POC Goal 3: Patient will achieve independence with home exercise program and breathing coordination techniques by discharge (target discharge [date]). Progress to date: Wife now present at sessions; education ongoing; wife demonstrating appropriate understanding."
