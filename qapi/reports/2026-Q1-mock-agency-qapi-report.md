## 3. Indicator Analysis

### 3.1 Acute Care Hospitalization — 🔴 RED

**Rate:** 24.0% vs. benchmark 14.7%
**Difference:** +9.3 percentage points above benchmark
**Trend:** Baseline quarter (no prior comparison)

**Analysis:** 
Sunrise Health had 12 patients hospitalized during their home health episodes in Q1, representing a 24.0% hospitalization rate that significantly exceeds the national benchmark of 14.7%. This elevated rate is the most urgent quality concern and is the primary driver for the Q1 performance improvement project. The 12 hospitalized patients represent 24% of the total patient population, well above acceptable standards.

Patient acuity factors and population characteristics contribute partially to this rate—with 72% Medicare patients and diagnoses including CHF (common in older populations), COPD, and post-operative status—but the gap is substantial enough to indicate opportunity for intervention in preventive care, care coordination, and patient/caregiver education.

**Root Cause Hypothesis:**
- Delayed clinical assessment and intervention on early warning signs (vital sign changes, symptom escalation)
- Gaps in medication compliance monitoring and patient education
- Insufficient coordination with primary care physicians on acute exacerbation protocols
- Limited use of telehealth or remote monitoring for high-risk patients
- Care coordination delays when patients contact ED rather than calling home health first

**Recommendation:** 
Initiate formal Performance Improvement Project targeting hospitalization reduction with focus on early warning sign recognition, enhanced care coordination protocols, and 24/7 clinical triage access for patients.

### 3.2 Emergency Department Use — 🔴 RED

**Rate:** 10.0% vs. benchmark 8.2%
**Difference:** +1.8 percentage points above benchmark
**Trend:** Baseline quarter (no prior comparison)

**Analysis:** 
Five of the 50 patients (10.0%) had at least one ED visit during their home health episode, compared to the national benchmark of 8.2%. While this gap is smaller than the hospitalization rate, it represents a preventable use of emergency services that correlates with higher costs and potentially adverse patient outcomes. ED visits often indicate either inadequate outpatient management or poor understanding by patients/caregivers of when to seek home health nursing versus emergency care.

The five patients with ED visits were distributed across the patient population and included diagnoses consistent with acute exacerbations (COPD, CHF). Without corresponding hospitalizations in all cases, this suggests some ED visits were managed outpatient, but the underlying preventive issues remain.

**Root Cause Hypothesis:**
- Patient/caregiver uncertainty about when to call home health vs. activate 911
- Gaps in after-hours clinical access and telephonic triage protocols
- Insufficient education on symptom recognition and early intervention
- Possible delays in scheduling nursing visits for symptomatic patients
- Limited availability of same-day or next-day clinical assessment

**Recommendation:** 
Launch targeted ED reduction initiative including patient/caregiver education, establishment of clear escalation protocols, and evaluation of after-hours nursing availability.

### 3.3 Timely Initiation of Care — 🔴 RED

**Rate:** 92.0% vs. benchmark 96.3%
**Difference:** -4.3 percentage points below benchmark
**Trend:** Baseline quarter (no prior comparison)

**Analysis:** 
Only 46 of 50 patients (92.0%) had OASIS SOC assessments completed within the required 14 days of admission. Four patients had delayed OASIS completion (SOC dates 15+ days post-admission), falling short of the 96.3% benchmark. While this 4-percentage-point gap may appear modest, timely initial assessment is foundational to care planning, risk stratification, and early intervention.

Delays in OASIS completion impact:
- Quality of initial care planning (missing early risk factors)
- Regulatory compliance and documentation standards
- Patient and referral source satisfaction
- Ability to identify high-risk patients for proactive monitoring

**Root Cause Hypothesis:**
- Scheduling challenges or patient availability issues
- Administrative/clinical workflow delays in arranging initial visits
- Possible gaps in communication with referring physicians
- Staffing constraints during peak admission periods (January and February had 35 of 50 admissions)
- Lack of process monitoring or escalation protocols for delayed SOCs

**Recommendation:** 
Implement process improvement targeting same-week SOC scheduling, establish trigger for administrative follow-up on any OASIS SOC beyond 7 days, and monitor with weekly reports.

## 7. Recommendations for Next Quarter

1. **Hospitalization Reduction** — Complete implementation of early warning sign protocols, establish 24/7 clinical triage line, and conduct staff education on care coordination with emergency services. Target: 2-percentage-point reduction by end of Q2.

2. **ED Utilization and Patient Education** — Deploy standardized patient/caregiver education materials for high-risk diagnoses (CHF, COPD); establish clear escalation pathways; measure ED utilization monthly.

3. **Timely OASIS Completion** — Implement weekly monitoring report of SOC scheduling and completion status; establish threshold triggers for administrative escalation; allocate assessment capacity in first week post-admission.

4. **Functional Outcome Optimization** — Review occupational and physical therapy protocols to enhance improvement rates for ambulation, bathing, and dyspnea outcomes; consider standardized assessment tools (e.g., dyspnea scale for COPD/CHF patients).

5. **Governing Body Engagement** — Present Q1 findings and PIP roadmap at April board meeting; schedule Q2 progress update for June meeting.
